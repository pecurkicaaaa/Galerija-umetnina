Johannes Vermeer (1632 - 1675)

Johannes Vermeer, also known as Jan Vermeer or Jan Vermeer van Delft (October 31, 1632 - December 15, 1675), was a renowned Dutch Baroque painter of the 17th century during the Golden Age of the Netherlands.

The complete body of Vermeer's work is relatively small and comprises 36 known paintings today. The formats of his paintings are quite small. In the early phase of his career, he painted historical scenes, but later he specialized in genre painting. From the perspective of contemporary aesthetics, his most famous works include "The Allegory of Painting" (also known as "The Artist in His Studio"), "View of Delft," and "Girl with a Pearl Earring." During the 19th century, due to the growing interest in his art and the limited number of known works, a considerably larger number of paintings were attributed to him. Through ongoing research, his unique painting technique has been discovered and studied. Johannes Vermeer is celebrated for his masterful use of light, intricate compositions, and attention to detail, which have made him one of the most respected and admired painters in the history of Western art. His works continue to captivate audiences with their beauty and the enigmatic allure of his subjects...

Gustav Klimt (1862 - 1918)

Gustav Klimt (July 14, 1862 - February 6, 1918) was an Austrian symbolist painter and one of the founders of the Vienna Secession. Gustav Klimt was born in Baumgarten, near Vienna, Austria, as the second of seven children to engraver Ernst Klimt and his wife Anna, née Finster.

His works encompass paintings, murals, drawings, and other artistic disciplines, particularly applied arts. Today, they are largely exhibited in the Vienna Secession Gallery and within the Belvedere Palace (Vienna), which has been transformed into a museum. Klimt's primary subject matter was the female body, and his works are infused with eroticism. A large number of his drawings are held in private collections of Viennese collectors, who cherish them as their greatest treasures...

Leonardo Da Vinci (1452 - 1519)

Leonardo di ser Piero da Vinci (April 15, 1452 - May 2, 1519), known as Leonardo da Vinci, was an Italian Renaissance architect, inventor, engineer, sculptor, and painter.

Leonardo is among the greatest painters in the history of art and is often considered the founder of the High Renaissance. Despite having many lost works and fewer than 25 attributed major works, including numerous unfinished pieces, he created some of the most influential paintings in Western art. The Mona Lisa is his most famous work and is often regarded as the most famous painting in the world. The Last Supper is the most reproduced religious painting of all time, and his drawing of the Vitruvian Man is also considered a cultural icon...

Michelangelo Buonarroti (1475 - 1564)

Michelangelo di Lodovico Buonarroti Simoni (March 6, 1475 - February 18, 1564) was an Italian Renaissance sculptor, painter, architect, and poet, widely regarded as one of the most famous and influential artists in the history of European art.

During his lifetime, Michelangelo was referred to as "Il Divino" (The Divine), and today he is considered a universal genius. His dedication to work and artistry gave birth to the greatest genius of the Renaissance. His most famous work is the detail of the fresco "The Creation of Adam" in the Sistine Chapel in the Vatican. Undoubtedly, Michelangelo is one of the most influential figures in Renaissance art. Even more than 450 years after his death, he remains one of the most renowned artists in the world...

Auguste Rodin (1840 - 1917)

François Auguste René Rodin (November 12, 1840 - November 17, 1917) was a French sculptor known for his lavish bronze and marble statues, and is considered by some critics to be one of the greatest portraitists in the history of sculpture.

His Gates of Hell, commissioned in 1880 for the future Museum of Decorative Arts in Paris, remained unfinished after his death, but two of his most famous statues emerged from it: The Thinker and The Kiss. Among Rodin's most renowned portraits are the monumental statues of French writers Victor Hugo and Honoré de Balzac. His popularity remains significant, as evidenced by the numerous copies of his works that are still being produced...

Alberto Giacometti (1901 - 1966)

Alberto Giacometti (October 10, 1901 - January 11, 1966) was a Swiss surrealist sculptor and painter of Italian descent, whose extensive, diverse, and distinctive body of work represents nearly all dominant art movements of the 20th century.

In 1931, he officially joined the Parisian Surrealist group. He created various engravings and drawings as illustrations for books by René Crevel, Tristan Tzara, and André Breton. He also wrote for magazines associated with this group. Restlessness, dreams, insecurity, violence—these are the motifs of sculptures from this period: "Cube," "Walking Woman," "Reclining Woman Dreaming," "Slaughtered Woman," "Cage," "Flower in Danger," "Object Unfit for Throwing," "Table," "Skull"... Most of his sculptures from his youth and surrealistic phase were cast in bronze and started within the first ten years of the artist's life...

Thomas Nast (1840 - 1902)

Thomas Nast (September 26, 1840 - December 7, 1902) was a German-American caricaturist and editorial cartoonist often regarded as the "Father of American Cartooning."

He developed his artistic skills in his youth and ambitiously sought to become a painter. At the age of 15, he applied for a job as an illustrator at the magazine Illustrated Newspaper by Frank Leslie, a highly popular publication at that time. Nast was associated with Harper's Weekly from 1859 to 1860 and from 1862 to 1886. His influence was so widespread that Theodore Roosevelt once said, "Thomas Nast was our best teacher."

Thomas Nast painted Santa Claus in a red suit in 1863, and the image was published in the weekly magazine Harper's Weekly with the idea of sending a Christmas message amidst the American Civil War. Later, Nast created multiple illustrations of Santa Claus, and this depiction of the holiday figure became recognizable. It was the first version of the modern-day Santa Claus as we know him today...
